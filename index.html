The Rowhammer Bug

Rowhammer is a vulnerability in DRAM devices that allows for attacks such as privilege escalation and sandbox escaping. Repeatedly accessing a row in recent DRAM devices can cause bit flips in adjacent rows, and attacks demonstrated and documented by the Google Project Zero team have used this behavior to gain kernel prileges on x86-64 Linux machines (from unprivileged user-land).

The x86 exploit utilizes repeated CLFUSH instructions to induce bit-flipping and gain read-write access to all of physical memory. However, the Google team believes this vulnerability exists on other machines and operating systems. This is because the vulnerability is a result of the increasing density of DRAM devices; since the individual cells are packed closer together, the electrical charge sent to one row has a greater chance of effecting the cells in adjacent rows as well.

What's vulnerable?

Specific vulnerabilites—in DRAM devices, CPU memory controllers, and BIOS devices—are not yet public. However, caution is advisable, as always, in running any binaries. Furthermore the Google team has speculated that this vulnerability may even be exploitable in both OS-level virtualized environments (x86 virtualization offers no control over CFLUSH instructions) and application-level virtualized code. Similar bit-flipping attacks were demonstrated in Java in 2003 by Govindavajhala and Appel at Princeton. Certain JavaScript features, such as typed arrays (effectively contiguous byte arrays), also seem likely to be exploitable.

Mitigations

ECC memory is likely much less vulnerable to this attack. Additionally the Google team found that efforts may be in place in LPDDR4 to mitigate the vulnerability, and that one DRAM vendor may have already integrated technology to protect against rowhammer within their DRAM devices.

However, given the that this vulnerability is still extremely new, the best course of action is to run your own tests of your system and exercise extra caution in which binaries you execute on your system.
